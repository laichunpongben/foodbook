The Osaka working-class dish that became a Japanese institution. Okonomiyaki has roots in *Funoyaki* — a thin Edo-period crepe with miso — but the modern form, with cabbage as the bulk ingredient and a sweet-brown finishing sauce, was a postwar invention when rice rationing pushed cooks to find a wheat-based meal that felt substantial. Osaka's *konamon* (flour-based dishes) tradition — including takoyaki, kushikatsu, and udon — comes from this same era.

The dish exists in a third regional form too: Tokyo *monjayaki*, which keeps the okonomi-yaki ingredients but uses far more liquid and cooks the result into a runny, hot-griddle-skim that the eater scrapes off with a tiny spatula and eats directly from the iron. Tokyo doesn't pretend it's the same dish; *monjayaki* is its own category.
