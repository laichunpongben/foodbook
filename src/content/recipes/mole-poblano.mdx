notes: |
  Mole poblano is the dish that introduces the rest of the world to the
  fact that Mexican cuisine has a complicated formal tradition older
  than most of Europe's. The classical version uses sixty to a hundred
  ingredients across multiple subcategories — chiles, fruits, nuts,
  seeds, spices, aromatics, thickeners, chocolate — each toasted and
  blended in sequence. The thirty-ingredient version here is the home
  cook's compromise.

  Day-two improvement is real. Day-three is even better. The sauce
  freezes well in 500 ml portions and is the highest-leverage thing
  to have in a freezer — drop frozen mole into a pan with any cooked
  poultry and you have dinner in 15 minutes.

  Origin myth: the dish was invented at the Convent of Santa Rosa in
  Puebla in the 17th century by Sor Andrea de la Asunción, who
  improvised a sauce for a visiting bishop. Like most origin myths
  it's almost certainly false; the dish is older and developed
  collectively. The story endures because it makes a good plaque on
  the convent wall.
